3.2 Three Structural Pressures

The landscape of web data access has undergone a structural transformation since approximately 2018. Three pressures — enclosure, exemption, and erosion — explain not just that access is shrinking but why, and what forces are driving the change.

3.2.1 Enclosure

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper Section X. Key points:]

  • Definition: The privatization of previously accessible data — platforms converting shared resources into controlled assets
  • Mechanisms: API shutdowns and pricing changes, removal of third-party archives, restrictions on data exports, data licensing deals for AI training
  • Cases to detail:
    • Twitter/X: from free Academic Research API to paid tiers ($42K/month for enterprise access), to effective shutdown of research access post-acquisition
    • Reddit: free API since 2008 → $0.24 per 1,000 API calls (July 2023), Pushshift removal, 7,000+ subreddit blackout
    • Facebook: post-Cambridge Analytica lockdown of Graph API, CrowdTangle shutdown
  • Historical parallel: enclosure of common land in England — how a shared resource was privatized, who benefited, and what was lost
  • The LLM accelerant: how generative AI made user-generated content valuable as training data (Reddit’s $60M licensing deal with Google), accelerating the incentive to enclose

3.2.2 Exemption

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper Section X. Key points:]

  • Definition: Platforms operating beyond meaningful regulatory scrutiny, invoking privacy, IP, or competitive sensitivity to resist independent investigation
  • Mechanisms: weaponizing the CFAA against researchers, selective deployment of “user protection” narratives, regulatory capture, lobbying against transparency mandates
  • Cases to detail:
    • Platform claims that API restrictions protect user privacy — while continuing to sell user data to advertisers
    • Resistance to researcher access under the EU Digital Services Act
    • The asymmetry: platforms can study their users at will, but independent researchers cannot study the platforms
  • Connection to Chapter 2: how the legal frameworks from Chapter 2 are deployed strategically by platforms

3.2.3 Erosion

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper Section X. Key points:]

  • Definition: The decay of the infrastructure for sustained observation — the gradual disappearance of the conditions that make web data research possible
  • Mechanisms: link rot, platform churn (Twitter → X), migration to federated/decentralized networks complicating basic measurement, reproducibility crisis as underlying data disappears
  • Cases to detail:
    • Studies showing that significant percentages of web links break within years
    • The challenge of longitudinal research when platforms redesign their HTML, change their APIs, or disappear entirely
    • The reproducibility crisis in computational social science: papers that cannot be replicated because the data source no longer exists
  • Connection to later chapters: the Wayback Machine (Chapter 7) as a counter-erosion infrastructure; automation (Chapter 14) as a response to data impermanence

3.3 Three Counter-Values

In response to these pressures, three values can guide how researchers, technologists, and institutions build and sustain access to web data for the public interest.

3.3.1 Openness

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper. Key points:]

  • Transparent methods and pipelines so others can evaluate and reproduce your work
  • Redundant archives that do not depend on any single platform’s cooperation
  • Methodological legibility: documenting not just what data you collected but how you collected it and what constraints shaped the collection
  • Durable public repositories (Internet Archive, Zenodo, institutional data archives)

3.3.2 Oversight

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper. Key points:]

  • Independent auditing of platform algorithms and content moderation
  • Documentation standards for datasets and collection methods
  • Impact assessments before and after data access changes
  • Regulatory frameworks that mandate researcher access (EU DSA Article 40)

3.3.3 Ownership

[TO FILL: Adapt from JRC paper. Key points:]

  • Collective governance over data preservation and access
  • Community-maintained infrastructure that resists centralized enclosure
  • Interoperable systems (federated protocols like ActivityPub and AT Protocol) that distribute control
  • Data cooperatives and community-managed archives

3.4 Public Interest Lineages

The tensions described in this chapter are not new to data science. Other professions have confronted analogous challenges — powerful actors controlling access to information that the public needs — and developed institutional responses.

[TO FILL: Brief treatment of each lineage. Key points:]

  • Journalism: Freedom of information laws, source protection, the tradition of investigative reporting as public accountability
  • Law: Discovery processes, transparency requirements, due process as protection against arbitrary power
  • Accounting: Audit standards, disclosure requirements, the fiduciary duty to stakeholders
  • Planning: Public comment periods, environmental impact assessments, community input on decisions affecting shared resources
  • Engineering: Safety standards, professional codes of ethics, the obligation to consider public welfare

[Author note: the goal here is a brief paragraph on each, not a deep dive. The argument is that data science is developing its own public interest mission and can learn from these precedents.]

3.5 What This Means for Practice

The rest of this book equips you with a diverse technical toolkit precisely because no single method of access is reliable. When APIs close, you may need to scrape HTML (Chapter 6, Chapter 8). When web pages change, archives provide continuity (Chapter 7). When documents are locked in inaccessible formats, PDF extraction opens them up (Chapter 9). When platforms restrict automated access, federated alternatives offer open protocols (Chapter 12). And when you need sustained observation over time, automation turns one-time data collection into durable infrastructure (Chapter 14).

The strategic lesson is redundancy and flexibility. Build your skills across the full range of techniques so that the closure of any single data source does not shut down your research program.
